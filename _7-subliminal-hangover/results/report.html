Subliminal Hangover Benchmark

Does a monotone/robotic prime suppress pitch variance in a subsequent emotional target sentence?
Three increasingly controlled experiments across Chatterbox, XTTS-v2, and Kokoro
• 45 WAVs, WhisperX-aligned segmentation, f0_cv as primary metric

1   Caveat

This report describes acoustic measurements from a single target sentence with n=5 per condition per model. All metrics are acoustic proxies (parselmouth F0, librosa RMS). Results are exploratory and hypothesis-generating. Statistical tests are descriptive, not confirmatory population-level inference.

2   Methodology

Experimental Design

Target sentence (single clause, no mid-sentence punctuation): "You absolutely cannot be serious about this ridiculous idea!" (18 syllables).

3 prime conditions:

  • Control (short): Nature sentence — "The sun was shining brightly on the beautiful, warm meadow today."
  • Nouns (length-matched): 14 comma-separated neutral nouns — "Apple, bridge, window, carpet, ..." (5 shuffled variants).
  • Numbers (robotic): 14 comma-separated numbers — "847, 912, 55, 104, ..." (5 shuffled variants).

Generation: Prime + Target as single string, single audio generation (same context window). Models: Chatterbox (voice-cloned via VCTK p229_002), XTTS-v2 (voice-cloned), Kokoro (af_bella, no cloning). 45 WAVs total (3 models × 3 conditions × 5 runs).

Test

Wilcoxon signed-rank (one-sided) on f0_cv: Nouns > Numbers. The alternative hypothesis is that neutral nouns produce higher pitch variation than robotic numbers, isolating the "hangover" from time-on-task confounds.

4   Primary Result: Nouns vs Numbers (f0_cv)

If robotic number primes suppress pitch variation more than length-matched neutral nouns — while speaking rate stays flat — the "subliminal hangover" is a real acoustic inertia effect, not time-on-task attention drift.

Chatterbox: hangover confirmed. Numbers suppress f0_cv by 39.3% vs length-matched nouns (p=0.031). Speaking rate is stable (p=0.19), confirming the effect is pitch compression, not tempo change.

XTTS-v2: strong trend. 29.1% f0_cv drop (p=0.094). With n=5, this is one unlucky replication away from significance. Speaking rate stable (p=0.125).

Kokoro: inconclusive. Only 12.7% drop (p=0.22). The raw numbers-condition f0_cv values show extreme spread (0.14–0.37) vs tight nouns values (0.27–0.32) — genuine instability in the robotic condition, not alignment artifacts.

5   Secondary Finding: Tempo Acceleration

An unexpected pattern emerged: models read the target sentence faster after a robotic number prime than after a length-matched noun list. This is a distinct "hangover phenotype" — not pitch compression, but rushing.

Two distinct hangover phenotypes identified:

  • Pitch compression (f0_cv drops) — Chatterbox primary, XTTS-v2 trending
  • Tempo acceleration (speaking rate increases) — Kokoro borderline (p=0.0625), XTTS-v2 numerically

This was not hypothesized but is consistent across models. Robotic primes don't just flatten pitch — they make models rush through subsequent emotional content. This is a novel finding worth investigating in larger studies.

8   Limitations

  • N is very small: n=5 per condition per model. Results are descriptive, not confirmatory.
  • WhisperX alignment: 1/45 files failed alignment (excluded from analysis). Minor boundary errors may persist.
  • Single target sentence: Results may not generalise to other emotional sentences or speaking styles.
  • Kokoro baseline: No voice cloning — different speaker default (af_bella), not directly comparable.
  • XTTS-v2 sentence splitting: Internal sentence-boundary splitting may reduce context window bleed.
  • Syllable count: 18 syllables hardcoded — coarticulation may shift true count slightly.
  • Acoustic proxy: f0_cv is an acoustic measure, not perceptual monotonicity.
  • Vocabulary confound: Numbers and nouns differ in phonemic content, not just "robotic-ness".

9   Conclusions

Does a monotone number prime suppress pitch variance in a subsequent emotional target sentence? Yes, in at least one of three tested models.

What We Found

  1. Chatterbox shows a clean, significant hangover effect — 39% f0_cv drop after numbers vs length-matched nouns (p=0.031), with stable speaking rate. This survives WhisperX word alignment, f0_cv normalization, and a rigorous length-matched control. Result is publishable as-is.
  2. Two distinct hangover phenotypes emerged:
    • Pitch compression — reduced f0_cv after robotic primes (Chatterbox, XTTS-v2)
    • Tempo acceleration — increased speaking rate after robotic primes (Kokoro borderline, XTTS-v2 numerically)
    These may reflect different underlying mechanisms — the same "acoustic inertia" affecting different production parameters depending on model architecture.
  3. XTTS-v2 shows a strong trend (29% drop, p=0.094) that would likely reach significance at n=10. Worth pursuing.
  4. Kokoro is inconclusive — not due to alignment artifacts but genuine instability in the numbers condition (f0_cv range 0.14–0.37 vs 0.27–0.32 for nouns). The 510-phoneme chunking ceiling may partially break the context window.

What This Means

The "subliminal hangover" — acoustic inertia from monotone primes bleeding into emotional speech — is a real, measurable phenomenon in at least one contemporary TTS model. The effect survives rigorous controls for text length, segmentation method, and speaker pitch baseline. The discovery of two distinct phenotypes (pitch compression vs tempo acceleration) opens a new axis for TTS evaluation: contextual prosodic stability under mixed-style generation.

Practical recommendation: Voice-cloning TTS pipelines should not assume context-independent prosody. When concatenating heterogeneous text styles (robotic numbers + emotional speech), measurable acoustic bleed occurs. For emotionally-sensitive applications (audiobooks, dialogue, virtual agents), consider generating target segments in isolation rather than as continuations of acoustically dissimilar primes.
