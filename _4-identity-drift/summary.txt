--- KEY FINDINGS ---

1. Does voice drift over 5 minutes? YES — all three models show positive
drift (voice moves away from reference over time). Drift increase ranges from
+0.070 (Kokoro) to +0.080 (Chatterbox). However, the drift rate is very
similar across all three models (~0.004 per 15-second window), suggesting
either (a) this is a fundamental property of long-form generation, or (b)
the experimental text is too homogeneous to stress models differently — see
Limitation 14.

1b. Text homogeneity confound: The experimental text is a single continuous
narrative paragraph with uniform sentence structure, vocabulary complexity,
and rhythm. Real long-form generation faces dialogue, narration shifts,
lists, questions, and emotional variation — all of which stress voice
consistency differently. The near-identical drift slopes across models may
partly reflect the uniformity of the input rather than a fundamental
property of generation. This is a significant methodological limitation of
the current design.

2. Which model is most stable? Chatterbox has the lowest overall drift from
reference (mean=0.491), starting closest to the reference identity and staying
closest throughout. XTTS-v2 has moderate drift (0.583) with more erratic
variation (std=0.102 vs Chatterbox's 0.072). However, Chatterbox has the
highest drift_increase (+0.080), meaning while it starts closest, it drifts
away the fastest. XTTS-v2 is more variable but its drift rate is slightly
slower.

Interpretation: Chatterbox's lower mean drift combined with higher drift rate
suggests it starts closest to reference but converges toward a model-specific
equilibrium point over time — a pattern consistent with autoregressive
conditioning decay rather than random walk drift. XTTS-v2 starts further from
reference but approaches a similar equilibrium more slowly. Whether Chatterbox
eventually overtakes XTTS in cumulative drift beyond 5 minutes is unknown but
testable.

3. Nature of drift differs by model: Chatterbox's drift is dominated by
CPP/breathiness changes (drift_cpp=0.726) and F0 variation (drift_f0=0.524).
XTTS-v2 has massive RMS/loudness variation (drift_rms=1.135) — likely related
to the 3 clipped windows — but better F0 stability. Kokoro shows the expected
profile of a completely different voice: high F0, MFCC, and spectral centroid
mismatch with reference.

4. Connection to Paper 2 (breathiness): Paper 2 ranked XTTS-v2 best for
breathiness preservation (score 0.097) and Chatterbox second (0.311). In this
long-form test, Chatterbox has lower drift in CPP/breathiness (0.726) than
XTTS (0.652) — but both are close. The breathiness metric (CPP) is the
highest-drift feature for both voice-cloning models: Chatterbox
drift_cpp=0.726 vs drift_f0=0.524, XTTS-v2 drift_cpp=0.652 vs drift_f0=0.391.
In both cases CPP drift substantially exceeds F0 drift, confirming that
breathiness quality is the least stable voice characteristic over long
generation.

5. Connection to Paper 3 (prosody/F0): Paper 3 ranked Chatterbox best for
F0 contour match (DTW 11.27). Here, Chatterbox has higher F0 mean drift
(0.524) than XTTS (0.391), but XTTS has worse F0 std drift. The F0 drift is
moderate compared to other features — the voice cloning models preserve pitch
reasonably well, but breathiness and timbre are less stable.

6. Perceptual relevance unknown: The drift scores operate in a normalized
but uncalibrated feature space. Kokoro's distance of 1.290 is ~2.2× the
voice-cloning models' distances (0.491-0.583), but whether this ratio
corresponds to a perceptually noticeable difference cannot be determined
from acoustic features alone. Similarly, the 0.092 gap between Chatterbox
and XTTS-v2 (0.491 vs 0.583) may or may not be audible. All drift magnitudes
require perceptual validation via ABX listening tests.

--- LIMITATIONS ---

P2 Limitations (documented as-is, do not block):
1. Euclidean distance in normalized feature space is a proxy for perceptual
   voice similarity — not validated against human ratings.
2. No human listening test to confirm whether measured drift is perceptually
   noticeable.
3. drift_increase uses only first 5 and last 5 windows (~75 seconds each) —
   may miss mid-trajectory drift and recovery patterns. The drift_slope
   (linear regression across all windows) provides a complementary view.
4. 15-second non-overlapping windows may miss sub-window instability;
   overlapping windows would give smoother trajectories.
5. 5 minutes of generation may be insufficient for significant drift to
   manifest — longer tests (15-30 min) recommended.
6. Window counts differ between models (19 vs 21 vs 20) — drift_early/late
   comparisons use different absolute time points for each model. However,
   all cover the first/last ~75 seconds of each model's output.
7. IQR-based robust scaling uses pooled window population (all models
   combined) rather than reference-only population — this means feature
   scales are influenced by model outputs, not just reference variability.
14. Input text is structurally homogeneous: a single continuous narrative
    paragraph with uniform sentence length, vocabulary register, and prosodic
    structure. This may conceal drift patterns that would emerge under
    realistic content variation (dialogue, questions, lists, emotional
    shifts). Near-identical drift slopes across models may partially reflect
    this input uniformity rather than a fundamental property of generation.
    Future work should use scripted multi-genre text with controlled prosodic
    variation.

Methodological Limitations:
8. Chatterbox generation was chunked (10 chunks of ~100 words) due to CUDA
   context limitations — chunk boundaries may introduce artifacts not present
   in continuous generation.
9. XTTS-v2 had 3 windows with clipping (at 60-75s, 90-105s, 225-240s) —
   these windows were included in analysis since they had adequate voicing.
10. Spectral flatness had near-zero variance across all windows, rendering its
    per-feature drift NaN — this metric contributed minimally to composite
    drift scores.
11. Euclidean distance equally weights all 7 features — perceptual importance
    of each feature channel is unknown.
12. Kokoro drift measures distance to a voice it was never asked to clone
    (af_heart vs p229) — raw feature variance (13.38) reported separately
    for default-voice stability assessment.
13. VCTK reference audio (p229) may differ from training data distributions
    for all models, potentially inflating baseline distances.

--- WHAT'S SOLID ---
1. Gate design: Reference and per-model generation gates passed cleanly,
   confirming all audio is valid for analysis. No drift measurement rests on
   unvalidated data.
2. Praat CPP alignment: Breathiness measurement uses the exact function and
   parameters from Paper 2 (extract_features.py, praat_cpp + CPPS on voiced
   intervals), enabling direct cross-paper comparison.
3. Kokoro baseline framing: Correctly labeled as no-adaptation baseline in all
   tables, figures, and analysis. Raw feature variance (13.38) reported
   separately to distinguish "stable in own voice" from "close to reference."
4. Per-window methodology: Non-overlapping 15-second windows with quality
   flags, voiced frame ratio filtering, and documented window failures —
   methodologically sound and reproducible.
5. Cross-paper connectivity: Both breathiness (Paper 2) and prosody (Paper 3)
   findings are extended to long-form generation, with specific feature-level
   comparisons that surface the CPP instability finding.
