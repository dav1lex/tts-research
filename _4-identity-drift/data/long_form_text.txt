The morning light filtered through the curtains as she sat down at her desk. She had been working on the report for three days straight, barely sleeping, surviving on coffee and determination. The numbers still didn't add up, and she knew that someone in the company was hiding something. Outside, the city was waking up. Buses rumbled past the window. A dog barked somewhere in the distance. She took a deep breath and opened the spreadsheet again. This time she would find it. This time she would not stop until she understood exactly what had happened to the missing funds and why nobody wanted to talk about it.

The spreadsheet contained thousands of rows of transactions stretching back eighteen months. She had already sorted them by department, by date, by amount, but nothing obvious jumped out. The discrepancies were subtle, almost invisible — small rounding errors on vendor invoices that anyone else would have dismissed as accounting noise. But there were too many of them. They followed a pattern. Every third Thursday, a payment to a company called Meridian Consulting appeared in the accounts payable ledger. The amounts varied between eight hundred and twelve hundred dollars. The service description was always the same: administrative support. She had called Meridian Consulting three times. The phone number was disconnected. The address on file was a mailbox at a strip mall in Newark.

She rubbed her eyes and reached for her coffee cup, only to find it empty. The clock on her desk read seven forty-two in the morning. She had been here since four. Rising from her chair, she walked to the break room and poured a fresh cup, watching the dark liquid swirl in the paper cup. Her reflection in the window looked haggard, her brown hair escaping from the loose ponytail she had tied hours ago. She didn't care. Not anymore. Not when she was this close.

Back at her desk, she cross-referenced the Meridian payments against the payroll database. Each payment coincided with a week where one particular manager, David Calloway, had logged unusually high overtime hours. David was the regional director of operations. He had been with the company for eleven years. Everyone liked David. He brought donuts on Fridays and remembered everyone's birthday. She had always thought he was genuine, but now the data was telling a different story.

She pulled up David's personnel file and his approved vendors list. Meridian Consulting was not on it. The company's procurement policy required all vendors to be pre-approved by two levels of management. Somehow, Meridian had bypassed every control. The invoices were approved by a single signature — David's. She cross-checked his login timestamps against the accounting system's audit logs. Every Meridian invoice was submitted between eleven at night and two in the morning, always from David's office IP address. During those same hours, the building security logs showed only one badge swipe — David's.

Her hands were shaking slightly as she compiled the evidence into a single document. The total amount was staggering — nearly ninety-four thousand dollars over eighteen months, funneled through a shell company that existed only on paper. She saved the file to an encrypted folder and leaned back in her chair. The morning traffic outside had grown louder. More buses, more cars, the distant wail of a siren. The city was fully awake now, and so was she. She had found the missing funds. She understood why nobody wanted to talk about it. David was protected — his brother-in-law sat on the board of directors. But the numbers didn't lie. The spreadsheet didn't forget. She had everything she needed.

She stood up, stretched her aching shoulders, and walked to the window. The sun had climbed above the buildings, casting long amber shadows across the street below. She thought about what would happen next. She would take the evidence to the CFO, someone she trusted, someone who had been with the company even longer than David. But she would also keep a copy for herself, stored somewhere safe, just in case. She had learned over these three sleepless days that trust was a fragile thing, easily broken by the weight of what people wanted to hide. The coffee had gone cold again, but she didn't mind. She had found the truth, and for now, that was enough.

At eight thirty, she gathered her printed reports and walked down the corridor to the CFO's office. Patricia Morrison had been with the company for over twenty years and had a reputation for integrity that preceded her. She listened quietly as the young analyst laid out the evidence, page by page, spreadsheet cell by spreadsheet cell. Patricia's expression remained unreadable until she reached the final summary page showing the total amount. Then her jaw tightened almost imperceptibly, and she removed her reading glasses and set them carefully on the desk. She asked only two questions: was the evidence verified, and was there any chance of error. The answer to both was clear. The morning sun continued its steady climb above the city skyline as the two women sat in silence, the weight of what came next settling between them like a stone dropped into still water.
