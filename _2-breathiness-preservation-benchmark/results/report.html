This benchmark measures whether voice-cloning TTS models preserve breathiness cues present in reference audio. The gate mechanism validates that reference clips separate breathy from neutral before model rankings are accepted.

Methodology

1. Reference Selection

All 62 female VCTK speakers were processed for sentences 001-010. CPP and HNR were extracted per speaker (best sentence by voiced ratio). Speakers ranked by combined CPP+HNR z-score. Bottom 3 = breathiest, top 3 = most modal. Paired by matching sentence.

3. Feature Extraction

Three acoustic proxies for breathiness were extracted per WAV file using Praat (via parselmouth) and librosa:

MetricTypeWhat it measuresBreathy direction
CPP (Cepstral Peak Prominence)PrimaryPeriodicity loss in cepstral domainLower
HNR (Harmonic-to-Noise Ratio)SupportingNoise energy relative to harmonic energyLower
Spectral tiltSupportingHigh-frequency roll-off (100-5000 Hz)Lower

Voiced frames are isolated via energy threshold + F0 pyin (librosa) with voiced probability ≥ 0.50. Only voiced intervals ≥ 0.10s contribute to metric means.

4. Gate Mechanism

Reference clips must pass three checks before analysis is allowed:

  1. CPP separates breathy from neutral with Cohen's d ≥ 0.80 and correct direction
  2. At least one supporting metric separates with d ≥ 0.30 and correct direction
  3. All paired breathy-neutral contrasts are directionally consistent

5. Scoring

Score = mean_scaled_abs_delta - mean_contrast_retention. Lower score = better. Distance uses robust IQR-based scaling. Contrast retention clips to [0, 1] per metric.

Key Findings

Finding 1: XTTS-v2 preserves breathiness best overall

XTTS achieves the lowest score (0.097) driven by the lowest mean scaled distance (0.615) and highest mean contrast retention (0.518). Its performance is consistent across all three reference pairs, suggesting robust breathiness transfer regardless of accent or speaker identity.

Finding 2: Chatterbox competitive on retention, higher distance

Chatterbox matches XTTS on contrast retention (0.510 vs 0.518) but has 33% higher absolute distance from references (0.822 vs 0.615). This suggests Chatterbox preserves the direction of breathiness contrast but with less absolute accuracy — the output breathiness is in the right ballpark but farther from the original reference values.

Finding 3: Kokoro baseline confirms no cloning = no retention

Kokoro has no voice cloning capability. All outputs use the same default voice regardless of reference. As expected, contrast retention is zero and distance is worst (1.448). This validates the benchmark's sensitivity — it correctly detects when a model cannot adapt to reference breathiness.

Finding 4: Gate mechanism works correctly

The gate rejected the first two attempts (synthetic references, then weak VCTK pairs) and only passed after objective CPP+HNR ranking identified extreme outliers. The final gate values (CPP d=-4.15, HNR d=-2.03) provide strong confidence that the reference breathiness signal is real and measurable.

Finding 5: Spectral tilt is unreliable as a breathiness proxy

Across all three pairs, spectral tilt direction was inconsistent (correct on pair 003, wrong on 001 and 002). This supports existing literature suggesting tilt is sensitive to microphone placement, vocal tract length, and phonetic content rather than breathiness specifically. Future versions should consider replacing it with H1-H2 or harmonics-to-noise ratio from Praat.

Caveats

  • CPP, HNR, and spectral tilt are acoustic proxies, not direct perception measures
  • Unmatched accent pairs (pair 002, 003) may introduce phonetic confounds
  • Minimum viable pipeline: 3 pairs, 3 models, 24 files — increase both for publication
  • Kokoro lack of voice cloning prevents fair comparison — include as baseline only
  • Single sentence per pair limits generalization to broader prosodic variation
  • Reference breathiness labels verified by perceptual listening check
